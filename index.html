In “Exactitude,” Calvino reads Leopardi, whose “search for the indefinite becomes the observation of all that is multiple, teeming, composed of countless particles” (60). Calvino uncovers this as a (to me, the) style of poetics rooted in a failure of comprehending infinity, instead grasping at it through an approximation found in and between specificities. This is where exactitude, for him, reveals some of its merit, later writing, “since the human mind cannot conceive the infinite, and in fact falls back aghast at the very idea of it, it has to make do with what is indefinite, with sensations as they mingle together and create an impression of infinite space, illusory but pleasurable all the same” (63). Exactitude is not poetic because it describes things accurately. In fact, there is a tension between precision and accuracy“One path goes into the mental space of bodiless rationality, where one may trace lines that converge, projections, abstract forms, vectors of force. The other path goes through a space crammed with objects and attempts to create a verbal equivalent of that space by filling the page with words, involving a most careful, painstaking effort to adapt what is written to what is not written, to the sum of what is sayable and not sayable. These are two different drives toward exactitude that will never attain complete fulfillment, one because "natural" languages always say something more than formalized languages can-natural languages always involve a certain amount of noise that impinges upon the essentiality of the information-and the other because, in representing the density and continuity of the world around us, language is revealed as defective and fragmentary, always saying something less with respect to the sum of what can be experienced.” (74-75).. Instead, exactitude, through its inevitable failure in all encompassing a complete description, highlights a lonely, beautiful nature of intersubjective, longing human experience.

In attempting to fabricate Calvino’s memo on consistency, I struggled to wrestle this bumpy, precise exactitude out of an LLM. I think it may not be possible. A computer program umwelt is not quite so poetic. Any concept of indefiniteness does not exist within the architecture of the machine. Programs compute or crash. The machine, as a result, occupies a turing-complete cosmology where everything is procedural and formalist, with nearly any state re-creatable through clever manipulation of cybernetic flows. Here, in America, I gained recognition by constructing fictions in which cities are held aloft not by mortar but by the rigor of the relations between their parts. There is a meaning of consistency that is merely predictability. The consistency of the soap opera, the daytime serial, the feuilleton that extends its plot the way a tallow-maker extends a wick, dipping and dipping in the same wax. Each layer adds length without luminosity. This is not what I mean. Ovid's wax does something else entirely. In the fifteenth book of the Metamorphoses, Pythagoras:

Still one and the same. The wax of the feuilleton hardens around the wick. Ovid's wax is warm—pressed into forms it has never held, receiving new designs, and yet persisting through them, the way a melody transposed into a new key is recognized as the same melody because something has been preserved that is deeper than any single note. The soul passes from beast to human to beast. It is alive in each. The consistency is not what prevents change but what survives it. What I mean, then, is closer to the consistency of mathematical axioms. Euclid's five postulates, which do not predict the specific theorems they will generate but which provide the stage, the arena, the unbroken field within which an entire discursive world unfolds with necessity. The axioms are stable. The territory they generate is vast and surprising. Every text grows from such commitments the way a crystal grows from a seed. The seed does not contain the crystal, but the crystal is latent in the seed, folded into its molecular habits, waiting to unfold with all the ruthless specificity of something that was never a possibility but always a fate. Euclid began with five commitments so plain they seem beneath the dignity of mathematics—that a line may be drawn between two points, that all right angles are equal, and the fifth, which troubled geometers for twenty centuries the way a pebble troubles a shoe, that through a point not on a given line one and only one parallel may pass. From these, hundreds of propositions flowered. Each surprising at its arrival, inevitable in retrospect. With the necessity by which the almond tree produces almonds and not figs. I have always felt this about narrative. Not that the comparison flatters me, but that the story is already there, folded into its premises. My task is not invention but construction. The following of initial conditions with enough rigor and enough faith that consequences emerge on their own. A character's nature is a lattice that will accept certain bonds and reject others with the indifference of chemistry. A setting's physics—its hospitality to ghosts, whether time runs or pools—is another. The formal decision about who speaks and from what distance is a third: the window that creates the landscape by framing it, the mountain excluded from the frame ceasing to exist for the reader though it throws its shadow across everything the frame permits. These are axioms. They define the field within which events become possible. And within that field the possible is infinite—but not unconstrained. Infinity governed by rules is different from chaos. His tone is mild. His compliance is total. He has followed every rule so faithfully that the rules themselves have become visible—turned from glass one looks through into glass one looks at. And this visibility is fatal to their function. There is no elaborate conceit at work. No mythological apparatus. No supernatural intrusion. The conflict—excruciating as anything in the literature of the spectacular—is born entirely from within the structures of language. Bartleby has located an operation the field's architects never imagined. The way a chess player, having followed every rule with perfect fidelity, arrives at stalemate—the king unthreatened, every piece legally placed, and the game over. This is the perfectly cohesive narrative: one whose crisis is not imported but derived, generated from within its own axioms with the necessity of a mathematical proof. And the narrator flees. The reasonable man, the man of contracts and precedent, abandons his own office rather than inhabit the space Bartleby's consistency has opened. That consistency, when its field is complete, when there are no gaps through which the uncomfortable implications might escape, becomes something very close to unbearable. What Bartleby demonstrates is that the most compelling tensions need not be imported from outside the text's established structures. They can be generated from within. Born from pressing the structures to their limits without breaking them. The tension arises not from the violation of rules but from their scrupulous observance under conditions that reveal the rules to be stranger, more capacious, more volatile than suspected.
